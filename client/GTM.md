# GTM

The exact target user is a founder-led or engineering-led team at a seed to Series A startup, roughly 5 to 40 employees, with a technical product and at least some recurring spend on AI subscriptions or APIs. The person I would optimize the product and messaging for first is the engineering manager or CTO at a 10 to 25 person startup. That person usually owns the tooling budget in practice, even if finance or the founder still approves the card statement. They feel the pain directly because their team keeps asking for “just one more AI tool,” and they rarely have the time to benchmark whether the stack still makes sense.

What they are doing right before they want this tool is not “searching for AI audits.” They are more likely to be searching queries like “Cursor vs Copilot for team,” “ChatGPT Team pricing,” “Claude Team minimum seats,” “AI coding tools for startup,” or “how much should my team spend on AI tools.” They are also reading threads on X, Hacker News comments, or Reddit debates where people compare tools by vibe, not by total monthly cost. That is the wedge: Stacklane gives them the spend lens they do not currently have.

The first 100 users should come from very specific founder-and-builder channels. First, I would launch on X with a strong before/after screenshot of the audit results page and a claim that is concrete enough to invite disagreement, like “Most 10-person startups are overpaying for at least one AI plan.” Second, I would post in Indie Hackers, r/SaaS, r/startups, r/SideProject, and relevant Hacker News threads when AI tooling or spend comes up. Third, I would DM operators in small startup Slack groups with a personal hook: “I built a free tool that tells you if your Cursor/Claude/ChatGPT stack is overpriced. Want me to run your stack through it?” Fourth, I would publish three short teardown posts: one about AI spend per developer, one about team-plan traps, and one about when discounted credits are actually worth the effort.

The unfair distribution channel is Credex itself. Most people can build an audit calculator. Very few can pair it with a real downstream product that directly captures the identified savings. Credex can also publish anonymized benchmarks from completed audits over time, which makes the content layer stronger and creates a feedback loop no generic landing page has.

If this works in week one, traction should not be measured by raw visits. I would want 300 unique landing visitors, 75 completed audits, 20 saved reports, and 5 to 8 Credex-fit consultations booked. That would be enough signal to show the product is not just interesting but actually useful in the part of the funnel that matters.
